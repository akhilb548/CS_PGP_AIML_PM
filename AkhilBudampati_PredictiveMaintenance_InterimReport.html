Executive Summary

Project Overview and Key Findings

Purpose of This Report

This interim report documents the first phase of the Engine Predictive Maintenance project — a machine learning initiative designed to predict engine failures before they occur. The report covers data registration, exploratory analysis, data preparation, and the initial model-building phase. It is intended for senior decision-makers evaluating the technical progress and business potential of this initiative.

Business Problem

Unplanned engine failures cost fleet operators and manufacturers millions of dollars annually through emergency repairs, vehicle downtime, and safety incidents. Traditional time-based maintenance schedules are inefficient — they either service engines too early (wasting resources) or too late (risking failure). This project builds a data-driven predictive maintenance system that uses real-time engine sensor readings to flag engines at risk of failure before a breakdown occurs.

Key Business Findings

  • Engine RPM is the strongest predictor of failure — faulty engines run at significantly lower RPMs (736 vs. 885 RPM on average), indicating mechanical stress or degradation.
  • Coolant Temperature anomalies are critical warning signs — extreme outliers reaching 195°C (vs. a normal range of ~62–90°C) signal imminent overheating failures.
  • The Gradient Boosting model achieves 66.6% accuracy with an 85.2% recall rate, meaning the system correctly identifies 85 out of every 100 faulty engines — minimizing costly missed detections.
  • All models and datasets are version-controlled on Hugging Face Hub, enabling reproducibility and future deployment.

Business Impact Potential

Deploying this predictive model in a fleet management or manufacturing context can reduce unplanned downtime by an estimated 30–50%, lower emergency repair costs, and extend engine lifespan through timely, targeted maintenance interventions.

2.6 Key EDA Insights & Business Implications
Synthesized Findings from Exploratory Data Analysis
🔴
Insight 1: Class Imbalance Requires Attention
63.1% of records are Faulty vs. 36.9% Normal. Without correction, models will be biased toward predicting faults. Stratified sampling was applied to preserve this ratio in train/test splits, ensuring representative evaluation.
⚡
Insight 2: RPM is the Primary Fault Indicator
Faulty engines run ~149 RPM slower than normal engines (736 vs. 885). This 17% RPM reduction is the strongest single predictor (r = −0.268) and should be the primary threshold in real-time monitoring dashboards.
🌡️
Insight 3: Coolant Temperature Has Extreme Outliers
Maximum recorded coolant temperature of 195.53°C is nearly 2.5× the mean (78.43°C). These extreme values represent genuine catastrophic overheating events and must be preserved — not removed — as they are critical fault signatures.
🛢️
Insight 4: Lubricating Oil Temperature Has Most Outliers
13.4% of Lub Oil Temperature readings are statistical outliers (IQR method). This high rate suggests frequent thermal excursions in the lubrication system — a leading indicator of bearing wear and oil degradation.
⛽
Insight 5: Fuel Pressure Elevation Signals Fault
Faulty engines show higher average fuel pressure (6.90 vs. 6.24 bar). Combined with low RPM, elevated fuel pressure indicates the engine is demanding more fuel to maintain speed — a classic sign of mechanical inefficiency or injector fouling.
✅
Insight 6: Features Are Largely Independent
Maximum inter-feature correlation is 0.31, confirming no multicollinearity. All 6 sensor features contribute unique predictive information, justifying their inclusion in the model without dimensionality reduction.

Why F1-Score as Optimization Metric? Given the class imbalance (63.1% Faulty), accuracy alone is misleading — a model predicting "always faulty" would achieve 63% accuracy. F1-Score balances precision and recall, penalizing both false positives (unnecessary maintenance) and false negatives (missed faults). For safety-critical applications, recall is particularly important as missed faults carry higher costs than false alarms.

A.1 — IQR-Based Winsorization
# Winsorize features using IQR method (cap outliers, preserve all records) def winsorize_iqr(df, features): bounds = {} for col in features: Q1 = df[col].quantile(0.25) Q3 = df[col].quantile(0.75) IQR = Q3 - Q1 lower = Q1 - 1.5 * IQR upper = Q3 + 1.5 * IQR df[col] = df[col].clip(lower=lower, upper=upper) bounds[col] = {'lower': lower, 'upper': upper} return df, bounds
A.2 — Stratified Train-Test Split

from sklearn.ensemble import GradientBoostingClassifier from sklearn.model_selection import GridSearchCV, StratifiedKFold import mlflow param_grid = { 'n_estimators': [100, 200], 'max_depth': [3, 5], 'learning_rate': [0.05, 0.1], 'subsample': [0.8, 1.0] } cv = StratifiedKFold(n_splits=5, shuffle=True, random_state=42) grid_search = GridSearchCV(GradientBoostingClassifier(random_state=42), param_grid, cv=cv, scoring='f1', n_jobs=-1) with mlflow.start_run(run_name='XGBoost_GridSearchCV'): grid_search.fit(X_train, y_train) best_model = grid_search.best_estimator_ mlflow.log_params(grid_search.best_params_) mlflow.log_metric('test_accuracy', accuracy_score(y_test, best_model.predict(X_test))) mlflow.sklearn.log_model(best_model, 'model')
